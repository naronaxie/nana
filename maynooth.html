About the project

This case study is an overview of the website design and mobile app for a fictional company called Maynooth Furniture. Maynooth Furniture is a new business selling affordable high-end design furniture made in Ireland. The goal of this project was to create an e-commerce website for people to browse and purchase furniture for home delivery. I was assigned to create the home page, category page, product page and contact page.

Empathizing with the user

I conducted a user survey of 4 participants between the ages of 25 to 75. The questions targeted the common experiences of users on other similar apps such as IKEA and Amazon. User surveys revealed the following results:

  • Individuals accessed desktop websites and mobile apps about the same amount.
  • Users typically gathered home decorating ideas from Pinterest and print.
  • Improvements users wanted to see were better shipping and better measurements and size context
  • The top features that were the most important to their shopping experience were free shipping and discounts/deals.
  • The secondary important features were shop by category and shop by collection.
Question: What device do you typically use to shop for furniture?

User Persona

Based on the client's needs and user surveys, I created a user persona that focused on the main goals and frustrations of our users.

Low-fidelity Prototypes

I began the design process by rapid prototyping. For this project, I wanted to focus more on the UI and visual design. Because the users primarily use Pinterest and IKEA for furniture design inspiration, I wanted to replicate the collage like photo gallery. I also created a separate blank page for a master navigation bar. Then I made the base foundations for how navigation between pages would connect.

Visual Design

When approaching the visual design for this product, I wanted to focus on a feminine color palette with simple, legible fonts. This features a pastel color palette with UI elements that were inspired by Pinterest. Other inspiring websites were IKEA, Target, and Amazon.

High-fidelity Prototypes

After creating a visual language, the low fidelity prototype was refined to focus on the main selling points for the client. An analysis of the user survey showed that participants are more likely to purchase items if they are offered free delivery and discounts/deals. In the high fidelity prototype, I focused on bringing these features towards the front page as well as a Pinterest inspired collage section for inspiration.

Mobile App Design

User survey data showed that participants use both website and mobile apps about the same amount when shopping for furniture. I created the mobile app to extend the audience's ease of purchase. The mobile app design focuses more on interaction design, as the way that participants use their mobile devices are far more interactive than their website counterparts. I added a swipe/drag feature for repetitive cards such as clearance deals and recently viewed. I also added more shadows so buttons appeared more clickable.

The orignal website design did not have a favorites button interaction animation, but was later added once developed within the mobile version.

User Testing and Outcomes

After creating the final interactive prototypes, I conducted moderated usability testing. The most important result of the study was that the "Add to Cart" button did not immediately read as a clickable button. Because the color was originally a neutral pink color, some of the users thought that it was a disabled button. The button was altered to be a more saturated pinkish-red color with a box shadow signaling that it is a call to action button.

Secondly, the free shipping banner did not stand out enough to users. Ideas for alternatives included a banner that was a constant at the top of every page or next to the product price text.

The overall design was successful, and the interaction design on the mobile platform gave users insentive to view more products than stagnant images on the website version did.

What I learned

This project taught me how important interaction design can be to make or break your mobile apps. Users seem to expect mobile apps to be more interactive as an industry standard, so it is important to research the current design trends.

Button colors are incredibly important and sometimes breaking away from the original color palette may be necessary. This user study was an important reminder to put visual heierarchy at the forefront of my design thinking when creating the UI of products.

Creating this project with a real company would have provided more concrete challeneges that I am interested in learning more about. The limitation of user testing a fake furniture company is the lack of pre-existing user data to study for the specific brand.

And finally, repetition is necessary. Repetition helps reiterate key points to your users in order to keep their attention and upkeep a predictable, reliable flow.
